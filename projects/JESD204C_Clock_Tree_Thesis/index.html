Master's Thesis

Experimental Characterization, SPI‑Controlled Reconfiguration, and Optimization of JESD204C Clock Trees

A systematic study of how PLLs, clock distributors, and SYSREF signals must be configured and measured to achieve deterministic latency and minimum jitter in high-speed data-converter systems.

RF Clocking JESD204C Signal Integrity LMX1205 HMC7043 SPI Jitter & Phase Noise · FAU Erlangen-Nürnberg · 2025–
Introduction

The Synchronization Problem

Modern phased-array radar, software-defined radio, and digital beamforming systems use tens or hundreds of ADCs and DACs that must all sample at exactly the same instant. JESD204C is the JEDEC serial standard that links these converters to FPGAs and DSPs at multi-gigabit speeds — but it imposes a strict contract on the clock that feeds each converter: low jitter, precise phase alignment, and a special synchronization pulse called SYSREF. Any violation of this contract produces link errors, non-deterministic latency, or a visible noise floor in the converter's output spectrum. The clock tree — the chain from a reference oscillator through a PLL and a fanout buffer to every converter input — is where this contract is enforced. This thesis characterizes that chain, reconfigures it via SPI, and optimizes it for minimum jitter and maximum timing margin.

The Jitter Problem — How Clock Edge Uncertainty Corrupts Samples
Fig 1 — When the ADC sampling clock has jitter, each edge fires at a slightly wrong moment. The ADC reads the analog signal at the wrong voltage — converting a timing error into an amplitude error. Reducing jitter is the central challenge of clock tree design.
The Standard

The JESD204C Link Layer

JESD204C is a JEDEC standard for high-speed serial links between data converters (ADCs, DACs) and digital processors (FPGAs, ASICs). Data travels over one or more lanes — differential serial links running at multi-gigabit rates. Each lane carries a stream of octets (8-bit words) grouped into frames, and frames grouped into multiframes. The multiframe boundary is the synchronization anchor: it is where SYSREF aligns all devices. Three subclass modes define how latency is handled: Subclass 0 gives no deterministic latency guarantee; Subclass 1 uses SYSREF to lock all devices to the same multiframe boundary, giving predictable, repeatable path delay; Subclass 2 extends this with driven SYSREF for even tighter synchronization. Most phased-array systems require Subclass 1.

Fig 2 — JESD204C data is organized into octets → frames → multiframes. The multiframe boundary is marked by SYSREF, giving all converters a common timing anchor. Subclass 1 is the standard choice for deterministic latency in phased-array systems.
System Architecture

From Reference to Converter

The clock tree for this thesis has three stages. A stable reference oscillator drives the LMX1205 PLL, which synthesizes a clean, high-frequency sample clock and eliminates reference oscillator jitter through its loop bandwidth. The LMX1205 output feeds the HMC7043, a 14-output clock fanout buffer that copies the clock to every converter input while generating and distributing SYSREF. Each converter receives its DCLK and SYSREF from the HMC7043 with individually tunable delay, allowing skew across the PCB to be corrected in software via SPI. The thesis characterizes jitter, timing margin, and link quality at each stage of this chain — and uses SPI-controlled reconfiguration to optimize without board changes.

Clock Source

The LMX1205 — Synthesizing the Sample Clock

The LMX1205 is a wideband, SPI-programmable PLL-based jitter cleaner from Texas Instruments. It accepts a reference oscillator input (10–122 MHz), multiplies it through an internal VCO that spans roughly 5–9.8 GHz, and delivers the output frequency at a user-selected division ratio via differential outputs in LVDS, LVPECL, or HCSL format. The key mechanism is the phase-locked loop: the phase detector compares the reference to a divided-down copy of the VCO output and drives the loop filter, which in turn steers the VCO until the two are phase-aligned. The loop bandwidth determines how much of the reference noise passes through versus how much is suppressed. Once locked, all operating parameters — output frequency, output format, bias current, output power level — are changed via SPI register writes without interrupting the output.

LMX1205 PLL Architecture — Feedback Loop from Reference to Output
Fig 4 — LMX1205 PLL loop. The phase detector compares the reference to the divided VCO output; the loop filter integrates the error and steers the VCO until both are phase-locked. The feedback divider ratio (/N) sets the output frequency. All parameters are SPI-configurable in-system.
Clock Distributor

The HMC7043 — Fanning Out to Every Converter ● Key Component

The HMC7043 from Analog Devices is a 14-output clock fanout buffer and SYSREF generator designed specifically for JESD204B/C systems. It accepts a primary clock up to 6 GHz and a secondary input (for redundancy), then copies and distributes it across all 14 differential outputs in LVDS, LVPECL, or CML format. Each output pair has an individually programmable coarse digital delay (full clock cycle steps) and a fine analog delay (picosecond steps, independent of clock frequency) — enough to compensate for PCB trace length differences between the shortest and longest copper paths. Critically, it also generates SYSREF pulses from its internal dividers, synchronized to the distributed DCLK. Its specified additive jitter is only 15 femtoseconds RMS (12 kHz–20 MHz), which means it adds almost nothing to the LMX1205 output. Bias current per output channel is also SPI-adjustable — higher bias lowers the noise floor at the cost of power.

Synchronization

SYSREF & Deterministic Latency

SYSREF is not a normal clock — it is a synchronization pulse that marks the start of a multiframe. Each converter receiving SYSREF uses its rising edge to reset its internal Local Multi-Frame Clock (LMFC), the counter that partitions the serial data stream into frames and multiframes. If all converters reset their LMFC at the same DCLK cycle, they will all interpret the serial stream identically — and the delay from an ADC sample to its appearance at the FPGA becomes a known, fixed number of DCLK cycles on every power-up. This is deterministic latency. The threat to it is SYSREF metastability: if SYSREF arrives too close to a DCLK edge, the converter's capture flip-flop may resolve to either state, and two otherwise identical boards may count frames differently. The thesis measures setup/hold margins at each converter input and uses HMC7043 analog delay tuning to center SYSREF in the DCLK eye.

SYSREF Alignment — Locking the LMFC Across Multiple Converters
Fig 5 — SYSREF (orange) fires once and resets the LMFC counter on every converter simultaneously. LMFC_A (green) and LMFC_B (blue) align to the same multiframe start. Setup/hold margins (shaded) must be met at every converter input — the thesis measures and maximizes these margins using HMC7043 fine analog delay.
Software Control

SPI-Controlled Reconfiguration

Every parameter that matters for clock tree performance is reachable over SPI — without touching the board. The LMX1205 register map controls output frequency, divider ratios, output format (LVDS / LVPECL / HCSL), and output power. The HMC7043 register map controls per-channel digital and analog delay, output format, SYSREF mode (Continuous / Gated / One-Shot), bias current (and thus the noise floor vs. power trade-off), and synchronization trigger. An embedded controller runs the configuration sequence: write all registers → wait for PLL lock (LMX1205 LOCK pin) → trigger SYNC → verify HMC7043 SYSREF Valid interrupt. The thesis automates sweeps of delay settings, output formats, and bias currents, logging measured jitter after each change to build an optimization map over the full design space.

SPI Configuration Flow — Power-Up to Verified Lock
Fig 6 — SPI configuration flow from power-up to verified lock. If the LMX1205 does not lock, the register write sequence is retried. If SYSREF is not valid (setup/hold violated), the HMC7043 analog delay is tuned and SYNC is re-triggered. Once locked, the outer loop sweeps all SPI-controllable parameters and logs measured jitter to find the optimum configuration.
Experiments

Characterizing the Clock Tree ● In Progress

Jitter is measured in both domains. In the time domain, an oscilloscope captures period jitter, cycle-to-cycle jitter, and SYSREF setup/hold margins at each converter input. In the frequency domain, a phase noise analyzer produces the dBc/Hz spectrum that reveals which noise sources dominate at different offset frequencies. To isolate the HMC7043's additive jitter, the LMX1205 output is measured alone, then re-measured at each HMC7043 output — the difference is the distributor's contribution to the jitter budget. The planned experiment matrix covers: (1) baseline jitter budget mapping across all outputs, (2) per-channel delay sweep to maximize SYSREF margin, (3) SYSREF Continuous vs. One-Shot comparison to quantify digital noise coupling, (4) output format comparison (LVDS / LVPECL / CML) and its effect on link stability, and (5) bias current sweep to map the noise floor vs. power trade-off.

Measurement Pipeline — From Clock Tree to Jitter Budget
Fig 7 — Measurement pipeline. The clock tree under test feeds three instruments simultaneously: a phase noise analyzer for spectral jitter, an oscilloscope for time-domain margins, and a JESD204C protocol analyzer for link stability. Results feed back into the SPI optimization loop to converge on the lowest-jitter, most-stable register configuration.
Summary

Why This Work Matters

A clock tree that merely delivers a clock signal is not enough. In a multi-channel ADC or DAC system, every femtosecond of jitter degrades SNR, every picosecond of SYSREF misalignment risks non-deterministic latency, and every decibel of phase noise can appear as a spurious spur in the converter's output spectrum. The conventional approach — board spin after board spin, measured with an oscilloscope until something works — is slow and non-reproducible. This thesis replaces that cycle with a systematic, SPI-automatable characterization and optimization procedure.

Starting from first principles (jitter, PLL phase noise, SYSREF timing requirements), the work maps all SPI-controllable degrees of freedom in the LMX1205 and HMC7043 to measurable outcomes — integrated jitter, SYSREF setup/hold margin, link error rate — and identifies the configuration that simultaneously minimizes jitter and maximizes timing margin. The result is a documented jitter budget, a set of proven optimal SPI register values, and a reusable measurement and optimization procedure applicable to any JESD204C system built around these two chips.

This work is being conducted at FAU Erlangen-Nürnberg, 2025–.
